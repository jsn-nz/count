What is PAYE?

PAYE stands for Pay As You Earn — New Zealand's system for collecting income tax and the ACC earners levy directly from salaries and wages before employees receive their pay. Your employer calculates and deducts PAYE each pay period and sends it to Inland Revenue (IRD) on your behalf, which is why most NZ employees never need to file a tax return.

NZ uses a progressive tax system, meaning the more you earn, the higher the rate on each additional dollar. You only pay the higher rates on income above each threshold, not on your full salary.

How PAYE is calculated

PAYE is worked out on your annual income using progressive brackets — each slice is taxed at its own rate. On a $60,000 salary:

  • First $15,600 × 10.5%$1,638.00
  • $15,601–$53,500 × 17.5%$6,632.50
  • $53,501–$60,000 × 30%$1,950.00
  • Total PAYE per year$10,220.50

That's about $196.55 per week — an effective PAYE rate of 17.0%, well below the 30% marginal rate. ACC, KiwiSaver and student loan deductions apply on top.

Who pays PAYE and when?

PAYE comes out of the employee's wages, but the employer does the work: they withhold the right amount each pay based on your tax code, file employment information with IRD every payday, and pass the deductions on to IRD.

Employers pay IRD monthly — by the 20th of the following month — or twice a month for large employers. As an employee you don't have to do anything; it all happens automatically through your payroll.

What is ACC?

ACC (Accident Compensation Corporation) provides New Zealand residents with cover for personal injuries caused by accidents. The earners levy is deducted from your pay to fund this scheme.

For 2026-27, the earners levy is 1.75% of your income, up to a cap of $156,641 in annual earnings. Income above this cap is not subject to the ACC levy.

What is KiwiSaver?

KiwiSaver is New Zealand's workplace retirement savings scheme. Employees contribute a percentage of their gross pay, and employers must contribute at least 3.5% on top of that.

From 1 April 2026, the minimum employee contribution rate increased to 3.5%. You can also choose 4%, 6%, 8%, or 10%. Contributions are deducted before you receive your pay and invested in your chosen KiwiSaver fund.

Student loan repayments

If you have a New Zealand student loan, repayments are automatically deducted through PAYE once your income exceeds $24,128 per year (about $464/week). The repayment rate is 12 cents per dollar earned above that threshold.

Your tax code changes to M SL (or equivalent) to signal to your employer that student loan deductions apply. Repayments go directly to IRD and reduce your loan balance.

NZ tax codes explained

  • MMain job, no student loan
  • M SLMain job, with student loan
  • SBSecondary job, income under $15,600
  • SSecondary job, $15,601–$53,500
  • SHSecondary job, $53,501–$78,100
  • STSecondary job, $78,101–$180,000
  • CAECasual agricultural employee

Most employees use code M for their main job. If you have a student loan, your employer will use M SL. Secondary jobs always use a separate code based on your total expected income for the year.

Holiday pay and casual loading

Casual and seasonal workers in New Zealand are often paid an 8% holiday pay loading on top of their hourly rate, instead of accruing annual leave. This extra 8% is added to gross earnings before PAYE is calculated.

For example, a casual worker earning $25/hr with 8% holiday pay loading has an effective rate of $27/hr gross for tax purposes. Count. includes a casual/holiday pay toggle so you can see your accurate take-home pay.

How to read your NZ payslip

Every NZ payslip must show your gross pay (before deductions) and list each deduction separately:

  • Gross payTotal before deductions
  • PAYE income taxDeducted by employer
  • ACC earners levy1.75% of gross
  • KiwiSaverYour contribution (3.5%+)
  • Student loanIf applicable (12%)
  • Net payYour take-home amount

Contractor vs employee tax in NZ

Employees have PAYE deducted automatically by their employer each pay period. They do not need to file a tax return in most cases — IRD squares it up at year end.

Self-employed contractors generally do not have PAYE deducted. Instead they pay provisional tax in instalments throughout the year, and file an IR3 tax return. Some contractors on schedular payments have withholding tax deducted at source.

If you're unsure whether you're an employee or contractor for tax purposes, IRD's employment status tool at ird.govt.nz can help.

Working holiday visa and PAYE

Working holiday visa holders working in New Zealand are generally subject to the same PAYE rates as NZ residents. If you are in NZ for more than 183 days in a 12-month period you are taxed as a resident.

Use tax code M for your main job. You may also be eligible to enrol in KiwiSaver and claim your contributions back when you leave permanently. Recognised seasonal workers under the RSE scheme use the NSW tax code instead and are taxed at a flat 10.5% with no end-of-year return required.
